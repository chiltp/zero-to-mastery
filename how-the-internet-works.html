How the Internet work?

1. Browsing the Web?

  1. User accesses a web browser (desktop web browser/ mobile web browser), types an URL Google.com into the location bar and hits Enter.
  2. We ask a question who's this Google.com fellow? And that question gets asked all the way down to our ISP - Internet Service Provider.
  3. IPS gets that request and they send that off to something called the DNS - Domain Name Service. Essentially it's a phone book has the list of all these URLs like Google.com and the addresses of them.
  4. DNS sends off that request back through the ISP and the website or the web browser, Google Chrome in this case gets "172.217.7.23" - an IP address.
  5. The browser receives this IP address, then sends off another request to the Google servers.
  6. Google servers send text files (HTML, CSS, Javascript) to the browser and the browser will render and display the HTML content, and execute scripts (if have).

2. The Internet backbone

Imagine the internet as a massive highway connecting different cities. Now, the internet backbone is like the main highways that connect all those cities together. It's a super-fast and reliable network that carries lots of internet data between different places.

Some examples of internet backbone providers include:

  • Viettel: Viettel is one of the leading telecommunication companies in Vietnam and operates a substantial portion of the country's internet backbone.
  • VNPT is a state-owned telecommunications company in Vietnam. They have an extensive network that serves as a backbone for internet services in the country.
  • FPT Telecom: FPT Telecom is a major internet service provider in Vietnam. They have developed their own backbone network and provide reliable internet connectivity to residential and business customers.

In simple terms, the internet backbone is like the superhighway of the internet, carrying data between different places at incredible speeds.

3. Traceroute

Traceroute is a tool that shows you the path your internet data takes from your computer to a destination, like a website. It helps you find out which routers or devices your data passes through and measures the time it takes for the data to reach each one.

Let's say you want to traceroute to "www.example.com". When you run the traceroute command, you might see something like this:

  1. 192.168.1.1 (Your home router) - 1ms
  2. 10.0.0.1 (Your ISP's router) - 10ms
  3. 203.0.113.1 (Internet Service Provider) - 15ms
  4. 72.14.203.99 (Google's router) - 20ms
  5. 216.239.41.27 (Google's server) - Destination reached!

In this example, the traceroute starts from your home router, then goes through your ISP's router, and further through your internet service provider's network. Eventually, it reaches a router owned by Google and finally reaches the destination server.
